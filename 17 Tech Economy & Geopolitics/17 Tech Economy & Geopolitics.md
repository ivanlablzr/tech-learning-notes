How technology creates and captures value — and how that value became the central arena of great-power politics. This domain follows the money up the whole chain (raw minerals → chips → hardware → software/platforms → cloud/data/AI), then the power layered on top (export controls, sovereignty, antitrust, standards).

> [!question] The five questions
> **Why it exists:** technology is now ~17% of world GDP and the commanding heights of the economy; understanding *who profits where* and *who controls what* explains modern markets and statecraft.
> **Problem it solves:** turns the technical stack ([[03 Computer Hardware]], [[14 AI]], [[09 Cloud]], [[05 Networking]]) into an economic and political map.
> **Depends on:** [[03 Computer Hardware#Overview|semiconductors]], [[14 AI]], [[09 Cloud]], [[Network Infrastructure]].
> **Depended on by:** real-world decisions — investment, policy, supply-chain risk, career strategy.
> **Interactions:** value chain → market structure → state power → back to the chain (subsidies, controls).

## The big picture (2025–26)

- **Tech dominates capital:** as of mid-2026, **14 companies exceed $1T**; the top of the list is **Nvidia (~$5.2T — first ever to $5T), Alphabet (~$4.6T), Apple (~$4.5T), Microsoft (~$3.1T), Amazon (~$2.9T)** — and except Berkshire, the entire top 10 is tech.
- **AI is the growth engine** *and* the risk: hyperscaler capex hit **~$725B for 2026 (+77% YoY)**, ~75% of it AI infrastructure, funded increasingly by debt — while critics warn of an **AI bubble / circular financing**.
- **Value concentrates at chokepoints:** one foundry (TSMC), one lithography vendor (ASML), one accelerator designer (Nvidia), three cloud providers, and one country's mineral refining (China ~70%) hold disproportionate power — which is exactly why this became geopolitics.
