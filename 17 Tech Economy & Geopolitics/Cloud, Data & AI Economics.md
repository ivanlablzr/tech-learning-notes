# Cloud, Data & AI Economics

The newest and fastest-moving link in the chain — and the one rewriting the rules. Cloud turned computing into a utility owned by three firms; AI then turned that utility into the most **capital-intensive** boom in tech history, dragging the "capital-light" giants into spending hundreds of billions on physical infrastructure. This note covers the money behind cloud, data, and AI.

## 1. The cloud oligopoly

Cloud computing is a **three-firm market**: as of Q1 2026, **AWS ~30%, Microsoft Azure ~25%, Google Cloud ~13% — ~68% combined**, on quarterly infrastructure spend of **~$129B (+35% YoY)**. Its economics are excellent:

- **Capital-intensive but recurring** — huge up-front data-center cost, then sticky, high-retention, usage-based revenue (the opposite of one-time hardware sales).
- **Economies of scale** — only hyperscalers can amortize global data centers, custom silicon, and networking; this is a structural barrier that keeps the market at three.
- **Lock-in** — data gravity, proprietary services, and egress fees make leaving costly; AWS is Amazon's **profit engine** even though retail is bigger by revenue.

Growth is now **AI-led**: Azure (+40%) and Google Cloud (+63%) are growing far faster than AWS (+19%), because AI workloads are pulling enterprises onto whichever cloud has GPUs and model access.

## 2. The AI capital super-cycle

This is the defining economic event of the moment. The four biggest hyperscalers plan **~$725B of capex in 2026 — up ~77% YoY** (Amazon ~$200B, Google ~$175–185B, Meta ~$115–135B, Microsoft ~$110–120B), with **~75% tied directly to AI infrastructure** (GPUs, data centers, power). Goldman projects **~$5.3T** of capex from these four across FY25–30.

What changed: the **cost base of computing shifted from software (cheap to copy) to compute (expensive to build)**. Training a frontier model costs hundreds of millions; serving it ("inference") costs real money per query. The capital-light platform model broke — these firms now look part-utility, part-industrial.

## 3. Compute & energy as the new scarce inputs

AI's bottlenecks are now **physical**:

- **GPUs** — Nvidia accelerators are the scarce, expensive input; access to them is a competitive weapon (and the target of [[US-China Tech War & Export Controls|export controls]]).
- **Electricity** — data-center power demand reached **~485 TWh in 2025 (+17%)** and is forecast to **~roughly double to ~945 TWh by 2030 (~3% of global electricity)**; **AI-specific** data centers grew **~50% in 2025** alone. Data centers are becoming a top-tier electricity consumer, straining grids and reviving interest in **nuclear** (including SMRs) and long-term power purchase agreements.
- **Land, water, transformers, fiber** — the build-out is constrained by grid interconnection queues, cooling water, and even transformer supply.

Compute, energy, and chips — not code — are now the inputs that gate AI progress, which is why **"who has the power and the GPUs"** is becoming an economic and strategic question.

## 4. Data as an economic asset

Data is the **feedstock** of the modern economy, but it's an unusual asset: **non-rival** (usable by many at once), **cheap to copy**, and valuable mostly **in aggregate and in combination**. Its economic roles:

- **Advertising** — behavioral data → targeting → the Google/Meta ad fortunes ([[The Platform & Digital Economy]]).
- **AI training** — large datasets are now a strategic moat; the scramble for training data has triggered **copyright and licensing fights** (publishers, artists, code).
- **Lock-in** — accumulated user data raises switching costs and feeds the network-effect flywheel.

Because data both powers the economy and threatens privacy, it's the most heavily **regulated** asset (GDPR, data-localization) — covered in [[Tech Sovereignty & Governance]].

## 5. Is it a bubble?

The boom rests on an assumption that AI demand will justify the spend — and that assumption is contested:

- **Circular financing** — a web of deals where chipmakers and clouds **invest in the AI startups that then spend the money buying their chips/compute** (Nvidia ↔ OpenAI ↔ Oracle/cloud, etc.). Estimated at **>$800B** of interlocking arrangements; critics warn it inflates apparent demand and could magnify a downturn.
- **Loss-making leaders** — frontier labs burn cash (OpenAI projected to lose **~$14B in 2026**); monetization lags the capex.
- **Debt-funded capex** — hyperscalers issued ~$108B of debt in 2025 to fund the build-out, and free cash flow is falling. If AI revenue disappoints, very expensive, fast-depreciating assets are left stranded.

The bull case: AI is a genuine general-purpose technology whose productivity payoff justifies front-loaded investment, like railroads or electricity. The bear case: it looks like a classic capex bubble with circular demand. **Both can be partly true** — transformative technologies have historically arrived *with* speculative bubbles (the telecom/fiber glut of ~2000 built infrastructure that later proved invaluable, after wrecking investors). The honest position is uncertainty about timing and magnitude, not direction.
