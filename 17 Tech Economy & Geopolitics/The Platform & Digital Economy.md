Above the silicon sits the layer that captures the most market value of all: **software and platforms**. A handful of firms built businesses with economics so favorable — near-zero marginal cost plus network effects — that they became the most valuable companies in history and de-facto regulators of digital life. This note explains *why* that happened and *how* they make money.

## 1. Why digital economics tip to monopoly

Three reinforcing forces push platform markets toward **winner-take-all (or most)**:

- **Network effects** — each new user makes the product more valuable to others (a phone network, a marketplace, a social graph). Value grows super-linearly with users, so the leader's lead compounds.
- **Near-zero marginal cost** — serving the 10-millionth user costs almost nothing, so the leader can scale globally without cost discipline forcing it to stop.
- **Switching costs & lock-in** — your data, contacts, files, app library, and habits live inside the platform; leaving is painful. Ecosystems (Apple, Microsoft) deliberately raise the exit cost.

Add **data feedback loops** (more users → more data → better product → more users) and you get durable dominance that ordinary competition struggles to dislodge.

## 2. The business models — how Big Tech actually earns

| Company | Core money engine | Economic logic |
|---|---|---|
| **Alphabet/Google** | **advertising** (Search, YouTube) | attention + intent data sold to advertisers; ~printing-press margins |
| **Meta** | **advertising** (FB/IG/WhatsApp) | the social graph monetized via targeted ads |
| **Apple** | **hardware premium + services** | brand + ecosystem lock-in; high-margin **App Store / services** rent |
| **Microsoft** | **software + cloud + subscriptions** | enterprise lock-in (Office, Windows, Azure); recurring revenue |
| **Amazon** | **marketplace + AWS** | retail is low-margin scale; **AWS is the profit engine** |
| **Nvidia** | **AI hardware** | sells the "picks and shovels" of the AI rush (see [[Semiconductor Economics]]) |

Two patterns dominate: **advertising** (Google + Meta take the majority of all digital ad spend — the "duopoly") and **subscription/recurring revenue** (the shift from selling a product once to renting it forever, which Wall Street rewards with high multiples for its predictability).

## 3. The platform rent — taxing the ecosystems you build

Once a platform owns the distribution channel, it can charge a **toll** on everyone who reaches customers through it:

- **App-store commissions** — Apple and Google historically took **~30%** of app revenue ("the Apple/Google tax"). This became the flashpoint of platform antitrust.
- **Marketplace fees** — Amazon's seller fees + ads now capture a large and rising share of third-party sellers' revenue.
- **Self-preferencing** — ranking your own products/services above competitors' on a surface you control.

This "rent extraction" is efficient for the platform and is precisely what regulators have moved against (§5).

## 4. Intangibles — the capital-light empire (mostly)

Classic Big Tech is **capital-light**: its value is **intangible** — IP, brand, software, data, network — not factories. That's why a software firm can reach a $1T+ valuation with relatively few physical assets, and why traditional accounting (which expenses R&D rather than capitalizing it) understates these firms. The major **exception is the new AI build-out** — suddenly the capital-light platforms are spending hundreds of billions on physical data centers and chips ([[Cloud, Data & AI Economics]]), a real break from the historical model.

## 5. The market-cap reality & the regulatory backlash

The result, as of mid-2026: **tech owns the top of the market** — **Nvidia (~$5.2T), Alphabet (~$4.6T), Apple (~$4.5T), Microsoft (~$3.1T), Amazon (~$2.9T)**, with **14 companies above $1T** and nearly the entire top 10 in tech. The **digital economy is ~17% of world GDP (~$20T)**.

That concentration triggered the **antitrust backlash** — the political economy's answer to winner-take-all:

- **US:** courts ruled **Google an illegal monopolist** in search; in the **Epic v. Google** case the Supreme Court (Oct 2025) let stand an order **forcing Google Play open** (rival stores, external billing) and pushing commissions **down from 30% toward 15–10%**. Cases against Apple, Amazon, and Meta are live.
- **EU:** the **Digital Markets Act (DMA)** designates "gatekeepers" and bans self-preferencing, mandates interoperability, and allows sideloading; the **Digital Services Act (DSA)** governs content/transparency. These are the world's most aggressive platform rules.

The throughline: the same economics that make platforms enormously profitable (network effects, lock-in, rents) are what make them targets — and the regulatory fights over **app-store fees, self-preferencing, data, and acquisitions** are now a permanent feature of the sector.
