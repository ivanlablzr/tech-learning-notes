# Tech Sovereignty & Governance

If [[US-China Tech War & Export Controls|the chip war]] is the open conflict, this is the broader, quieter shift: states everywhere are trying to **reclaim control** over the technologies they've come to depend on — silicon, data, standards, cloud, minerals, energy — and to **govern** the firms that run them. The result is a world fragmenting into **regulatory and infrastructural blocs**.

## 1. Semiconductor sovereignty — bringing fabs home

The pandemic chip shortage + the Taiwan risk turned **"who makes our chips"** into national-security policy, triggering an industrial-policy wave (the world hadn't done this at scale in decades):

| Program | Scale & status |
|---|---|
| **US CHIPS Act** (2022) | ~$52B subsidies + tax credits → **TSMC Arizona now in volume production** (Q4 2025, ~90–100k wafers/mo; $165B total commitment, a six-fab "gigafab"; 3nm by H2 2027); Intel/Micron/Samsung fabs |
| **EU Chips Act** (2023) → **Chips Act 2.0** (2026) | catalyzed ~€69B; the 20%-global-share goal is unrealistic, so **2.0 (June 2026) pivots to demand-side** support; part of the **EU Technological Sovereignty Package** (with **CADA** to triple EU datacenter capacity, an Open-Source strategy) |
| **Japan / South Korea / India / China** | Japan bankrolled **Rapidus** (2nm ambition) + lured TSMC to Kumamoto; Korea's mega-clusters; India's incentives; China's "Big Fund" |

The hard truth: **money alone isn't enough** — leading-edge fabs need ASML tools, TSMC-grade process know-how, talent, and supply chains that take a decade to build. Reshoring buys *geographic insurance*, not independence.

## 2. Data sovereignty — who controls the bits

Data became a regulated, bordered asset. The core idea: a nation's citizens' data should fall under its laws and ideally stay on its soil (**data localization**).

- **EU — the regulatory superpower:** **GDPR** set the global privacy template (the "Brussels Effect" — firms apply it everywhere); the **Schrems** rulings repeatedly broke EU↔US data-transfer deals over US surveillance; **sovereign cloud** offerings and the **EU Tech Sovereignty Package (June 2026)** push to cut reliance on US hyperscalers.
- **China — control model:** **PIPL** + Data Security Law + Cybersecurity Law mandate localization and **government approval to export data** ([[East-Asia Digital Infrastructure|the GFW economy]]).
- **Everyone else:** India (DPDP Act + localization), Russia (strict localization), plus a patchwork of national rules — making global data flows a compliance minefield.

## 3. Standards as power

Whoever writes the standards captures the rents and the leverage. Control points: **3GPP** (5G/6G — the Huawei fight was really about *standards-essential patents* and influence), **IEEE** (Wi-Fi, Ethernet), **IETF** (internet protocols), and the **ITU** (UN body). The deep contest is **governance philosophy**:

- the **multistakeholder, open** model (IETF/ICANN — engineers, industry, civil society), vs
- the **multilateral, state-controlled** model China and Russia push at the ITU ("internet sovereignty" / **"China Standards 2035"** / proposals like "New IP").

The winner shapes whether the future internet is open or state-partitioned.

## 4. Governing the platforms — antitrust & regulation

States are also reasserting control over their own [[The Platform & Digital Economy|tech giants]]:

- **EU:** the **Digital Markets Act** (forces "gatekeepers" to allow sideloading, ban self-preferencing, interoperate) + **Digital Services Act** (content/transparency) — the world's most aggressive platform rules.
- **US:** courts ruled **Google an illegal monopolist** in search; **Epic v. Google (SCOTUS Oct 2025)** forced the Play Store open and cut app-store fees; live cases against Apple, Amazon, Meta.
- **AI governance:** the **EU AI Act** (risk-tiered, the first comprehensive AI law) vs the US's lighter, innovation-first posture vs China's control-first approach — a third axis of regulatory divergence.

## 5. Resources & infrastructure as statecraft

The contest reaches down to physical inputs:

- **Critical minerals** — China's ~70% refining dominance and its **2023 gallium/germanium + Oct 2025 rare-earth controls** spurred Western "friend-shoring" (the Minerals Security Partnership, new mines/refineries) that will take years to bite.
- **Energy** — AI's surging power demand (datacenters heading to **~3% of global electricity by 2030**, see [[Cloud, Data & AI Economics]]) makes **grid capacity, nuclear, and energy policy** a competitive constraint and a sovereignty concern.
- **Submarine cables** — landing-station security reviews, the FCC blocking China-linked cables, and aid-funded cables to pre-empt Chinese ones (Coral Sea) make the physical internet a strategic theater ([[Submarine Cables]], [[Oceania Digital Infrastructure]]).

## 6. The three-bloc world

The trajectory is a **partial fragmentation of the global tech order into three poles**, each with its own model:

| Bloc | Model | Hallmark |
|---|---|---|
| **United States** | market-led, innovation-first, lighter regulation | owns the platforms, chips IP, and clouds |
| **European Union** | rights-and-rules-first ("regulatory superpower") | GDPR/DMA/AI Act; sovereignty via regulation, weak on building |
| **China** | state-led, control-first, self-reliant | GFW, data control, indigenization, standards push |

Most other countries pick and mix, or get pulled into one orbit (via Huawei gear, cloud regions, cables, or rules). The open question of the decade: **does the internet/tech economy stay one interoperable system, or split into walled blocs** — and what that costs in growth, security, and freedom.
