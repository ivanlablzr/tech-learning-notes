RIR: **APNIC**. Two advanced, geographically-isolated economies (Australia, New Zealand) plus a scatter of Pacific island nations whose connectivity is shaped by **cable subsidy and US–China geopolitics** — and increasingly rescued by Starlink. (Part of [[Internet & Infrastructure]].)

## Australia

Penetration ~96%; regulators **ACMA/ACCC**. Defined by the **NBN** — a **A$57B government wholesale fixed-broadband network**; all retail ISPs lease from NBN Co rather than build last-mile (the decade-long FTTP-vs-FTTN political saga is now reverting to FTTP). Carriers: **Telstra** (AS1221, ex-monopoly, fully privatized 2022, owns 50% of Southern Cross + an international backbone), **Optus** (Singtel-owned), TPG, and fast-growing reseller **Aussie Broadband**. As a geographically isolated continent it has invested in **cable diversity** — **Southern Cross / SCN**, **Hawaiki**, Telstra Endeavour, INDIGO (Perth→Singapore, fixing the latency of the world's most remote major city) — and is a huge **Starlink** market for the outback. It was the **first country to ban Huawei from 5G** (Aug 2018, ahead of the US/UK), on ASD advice.

## New Zealand

Penetration ~95%; regulator **Commerce Commission**. A clean **structural-separation** model: incumbent split (2011) into retail **Spark** and wholesale fiber **Chorus**, which runs the **UFB FTTP network** to ~87% of the population — all retailers lease equally. Internationally it is **cable-dependent** on **Southern Cross** + Hawaiki; the 2012 collapse of the **Pacific Fibre** direct-to-US cable left it more exposed than planned. Its Huawei stance is more nuanced than Australia's (managed restriction, not outright ban) given trade ties to China.

## The Pacific islands — cables, subsidy & geopolitics

For tiny, ocean-dispersed nations, a single cable is the lifeline — and a geopolitical chess piece:

- **Economics:** a cable to Tuvalu (~11,000 people) costs ~$50–100M — **$5,000–10,000 per person**; most Pacific cables are **aid-funded** (Australia, NZ, US, Japan), not commercially viable.
- **US–China competition:** Australia funded the **Coral Sea Cable** (2019) to PNG/Solomons specifically to **block a Chinese-backed cable**; the US funded the East Micronesia Cable as a China alternative (2021); the 2022 Solomons–China security pact raised Pacific-wide alarm. Cable governance has become a strategic contest.
- **Fragility — Tonga 2022:** the Hunga Tonga eruption/tsunami severed Tonga's **only** cable, causing a **5-week total internet blackout**; emergency comms ran on one Inmarsat link and deployed **Starlink** — the canonical lesson in single-cable risk.
- **Strategic nodes:** **Guam** (US territory) is a top trans-Pacific cable **waystation** (FASTER, etc.) with major military/SIGINT significance; **Fiji** sits on Southern Cross with outsized importance for its size.

> **Starlink as equalizer:** LEO latency (~30–50 ms vs 600+ ms geostationary) and capacity make real internet viable across the Pacific, and several nations have authorized it with minimal friction — the most consequential connectivity shift for the region's outer islands.

Connectivity tiers: **advanced** (Australia, NZ — multiple cloud regions, dense IXs, 10+ cables) → improving (PNG, Samoa, Tonga, Solomons — post-2015 cables) → **satellite-dependent** (Kiribati, Tuvalu, Wallis & Futuna, most outer atolls).
